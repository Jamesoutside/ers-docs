Northern Health Authority covers Northern BC — Dawson Creek, Prince George, Prince Rupert, Fort St. John, and dozens of smaller communities. The Goal — and How We Get There

A Master Lease Agreement — Northern Health commits to a fixed number of units at the Dawson Creek hub for their travelling staff. Guaranteed capacity, one invoice, one partner. But this is bigger than a lease. The master lease is the foundation of a full infrastructure partnership: purpose-built facilities that ERS develops and owns, additional revenue services layered on top (clinical spaces, community programming, daycare, transportation, concierge), and a long-term relationship that solves Northern Health's housing problem while generating exceptional returns for ERS. The more Northern Health commits, the more value ERS delivers — and the more profitable the asset becomes.

Once signed (or clearly in progress), it unlocks: development financing → architectural plans → builder → investor → growth.

But this is not a pitch campaign. NH hasn't been brought to the decision point yet. This is an extraction and education campaign — structured conversations with the CEO, board, and executives that pull out the information ERS needs and help NH understand the solution ERS has built. The LOI and master lease are the output of those conversations. Not the starting point. See the Engagement Tracks tab for the full conversation blueprint.

Messaging Framework

How We Talk to Northern Health

Every email, every slide, every conversation should come back to at least one of these three headline messages.

The Three Headline Messages

1
For: CEO, Board, Regional Directors

"You have a new hospital. You need housing to run it."

The Dawson Creek hospital is a massive capital investment. Without stable workforce housing adjacent to it, the investment underperforms. ERS is the housing infrastructure layer that makes the hospital operate at full capacity.

2
For: VP HR, Facilities, Housing Coordinators

"Your team is spending time on this that they shouldn't be."

Hotel bookings, accommodation disputes, staff housing complaints, turnover tied to unstable housing — this is administrative weight that ERS lifts entirely. One invoice. One partner. 24/7 support that isn't their problem anymore.

3
For: All audiences, especially Steve Raper and the Board

"We're not a hotel. We're a community."

Hotels tried. Hotels failed. ERS is grassroots — locally embedded, community-connected, purpose-built for healthcare workers. We know what it feels like to be far from home. We built something that fixes that.

Do's and Don'ts

✓ Always Say

  • "We are workforce housing infrastructure"
  • "Grassroots, community-embedded"
  • "We serve 6 hospitals across BC"
  • "One invoice, one partner, 24/7 support"
  • "We built this because we lived this"
  • "The Dawson Creek hospital is opening. The timing is now."

✗ Never Say

  • "We're like Airbnb for healthcare workers"
  • "We're more affordable than hotels" (leads with price)
  • "We have 34 units" (too small-sounding)
  • "We're a startup"
  • Anything with hotel imagery or language
  • "Partnership opportunity" (overused, meaningless)

Audience-Specific Angles

AudienceLead WithTheir #1 Concern
CEO "The Dawson Creek hospital is a once-in-a-decade asset. ERS is the layer that makes it work at full capacity." Does this move the needle on staffing crisis? Is this partner credible?
Board of Directors "ERS reduces Northern Health's biggest operational risk — workforce instability." Governance, fiduciary duty, community responsibility. Vendor pitch = wrong register.

CFO / Finance Hotel spend per person vs ERS rate. The delta. One invoice vs 40. What does it cost? What's the commitment? What's the risk?

Handling Objections

"We already have hotel arrangements."

"We've heard that from every health authority we work with before we started. What we've consistently found is that hotel arrangements solve the booking problem — they don't solve the belonging problem. Workers who feel housed feel different than workers who feel billeted. That's the gap we fill, and it's why retention improves when staff move from hotels to ERS."

"We need to think about it / run it through process."

"Absolutely — we'd expect nothing less. What we'd like to do is make sure you have everything you need to make that process easy. Can we put together a summary document for whoever else needs to review this? And is there a board meeting or planning cycle we should be aware of so our timing is useful rather than disruptive?"
