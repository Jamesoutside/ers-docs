Your Mandate

What we're asking you to do — and why it matters.

We are seeking Phase 1 and Phase 2 acceptance into the NRCan Offsite Construction Challenge — a federal program under Natural Resources Canada that funds offsite prefabricated wood construction projects. Our project qualifies on every dimension. What we need from you is the government relations work to get it accepted.

The Three Asks

1️⃣
Influence NRCan program officers and senior officials to accept our Phase 1 application

The Offsite Construction Challenge is administered by NRCan's Investments in Forest Industry Transformation (IFIT) / Green Construction through Wood (GCWood) team. We need relationship-level access to the people who evaluate and recommend applications — and a credible voice making our case before and during review.

Build political support at the ministerial and departmental level

NRCan sits under the Minister of Energy and Natural Resources. We need champions inside government — MPs, ministerial staff, and departmental officials — who understand why this project matters and will advocate for its acceptance. The healthcare angle, the rural BC angle, and the BC forest industry angle all have political resonance. Use them.

Position ERS as the answer to a documented, federal-scale problem

This is not just about one grant. The healthcare workforce housing crisis in rural Canada is a national policy conversation. We want to be known — at the federal level — as the company that has built the solution. Your work should advance both the immediate application and our long-term positioning as a federal infrastructure partner.

The Crisis

The problem you're solving — in evidence.

The healthcare staffing crisis in northern BC is documented, verifiable, and worsening. This is the narrative foundation of your advocacy work. Every conversation with a government official should come back to this reality — because it's not framing, it's fact.

The Deficit Spiral — How It Works and What Breaks It

Vacancies force Northern Health to hire agency nurses at 2–3× the permanent hourly rate.

Agency costs drove NH's operating deficit from near-zero in 2021–22 to $39.2M by FY2024–25.

Workers won't fill permanent positions in northern communities when there's nowhere to live — so the agency cycle continues.

ERS breaks the cycle. Stable, managed, community-embedded housing enables permanent staff retention — reducing the structural agency dependency that's draining NH's budget.

The New Hospital Makes It More Urgent, Not Less

The new Dawson Creek & District Hospital — $433M, 50% larger than the existing facility — is in final fit-out and opening in 2026–27. A larger hospital requires proportionally more staff. Without workforce housing, it will open at under-capacity. The ERS Hub is timed to open with it — one block away.

The Housing–Childcare Pairing — A Government Hook

In every year from 2022–2025, the NH Board has listed housing and childcare together as a paired retention barrier. Staff won't move to rural communities without both. The ERS Hub includes a licensed childcare facility for 50–60 children, operated by YMCA of Northern BC — addressing both barriers in one project.

Government angle: This project simultaneously addresses housing affordability, rural healthcare access, and childcare availability — three active federal policy priorities in a single build.

Messaging Framework

How to talk about this — by audience.

Every conversation should anchor to at least one of these three headline narratives. Adapt the language to the audience — the evidence is the same, the emphasis shifts.

For NRCan Program Officers & Senior Officials
"This is what the Offsite Construction Challenge was designed for."
BC-manufactured mass timber, replicable prototype design, demonstrated demand in an underserved rural community, leveraged capital stack. Tick every box. Then layer in the healthcare angle — a project that activates the BC forest industry AND addresses a documented national healthcare infrastructure gap is exceptional value for the program dollar.
For Political Stakeholders & Ministerial Offices
"65,000 northern BC residents deserve reliable emergency care. This is the infrastructure that makes it possible."
ER closures. A brand-new hospital that can't run at capacity. A $39M health authority deficit driven by avoidable agency costs. This is a healthcare access story with a federal infrastructure solution. Politically, this plays in multiple ridings and resonates with rural Canada — a constituency every party actively courts.
For Cross-Departmental & Policy Audiences
"One project. Three federal priorities: housing, healthcare, and forest industry."
CMHC cares about housing supply. Health Canada cares about rural healthcare access and workforce retention. NRCan cares about forest industry activation and sustainable construction. This project delivers on all three simultaneously. That is rare, and it deserves cross-departmental championing.
For Any Audience — The One-Liner
"A hospital cannot run without healthcare workers. Healthcare workers cannot stay without housing. Elias Range Stays builds the housing."
This is the simplest, most resonant version of the argument. Use it to open. Follow it with whichever evidence is most relevant to the specific person you're talking to.

Language — Do's and Don'ts

✓ Always Use

  • "Workforce housing infrastructure"
  • "Purpose-built, professionally managed"
  • "Offsite prefabricated wood — BC-manufactured"
  • "Replicable prototype — five communities"
  • "Grassroots, community-embedded"
  • "One block from the new Dawson Creek hospital"
  • "Northern Health has been trying to solve this for three years"
  • "The federal government has invested $433M in the hospital — we're the layer that makes it work"

✗ Never Say

  • "We're like Airbnb for healthcare workers"
  • "We're a startup" or "we're a small company"
  • "We're more affordable than hotels" (leads with price, not value)
  • "Partnership opportunity" (overused, meaningless)
  • Anything that sounds like a pitch rather than a policy solution
  • "We need the money" — frame as infrastructure gap, not funding ask
