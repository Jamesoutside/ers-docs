Executive Summary

What we are asking. Why it matters. Why now.

Elias Range Stays is seeking advocacy support for federal acceptance of the Dawson Creek Healthcare Housing Hub under the Natural Resources Canada Offsite Construction Challenge — Phase 1 and Phase 2. This brief provides the evidence base for that advocacy: the documented regional crisis, the Northern Health Board's own stated needs, and the precise alignment of our project with both federal program objectives and on-the-ground community demand.

The Argument in Five Steps

1

Dawson Creek's hospital is in crisis. Emergency department closures, nursing vacancies of 20–60%, and a $39.2M Northern Health operating deficit driven by agency staffing costs have created a documented, verifiable emergency in healthcare access for 65,000+ residents of the Peace River region.

2

The cause is explicitly housing. Northern Health's own board documents — 31 meetings from 2021–2026 — confirm that housing is the primary barrier to healthcare recruitment and retention in the region. Not pay. Not working conditions. Housing.

Our project is the exact solution they have been asking for. One block from the new $433M Dawson Creek hospital. 32 purpose-built healthcare workforce units. A licensed childcare facility. Full-service operation. Offsite prefabricated wood construction — BC-manufactured, replicable, sustainable.

5

Federal investment here is multiplied across the entire northern BC healthcare system. This is designed as a prototype — five communities, five hubs. Every dollar invested in Phase 1 unlocks a replicable template at Fort St. John, Smithers, Terrace, and Prince George. The federal government's investment in this project is an investment in a national housing and healthcare model.

The Problem

Dawson Creek's Healthcare Crisis: A Region at Breaking Point

This is not a future risk. It is a present failure. Residents of the Peace River region are losing access to emergency care, specialist services, and basic healthcare because hospitals cannot retain the staff they need. The documented cause — confirmed by Northern Health's own board — is a lack of adequate housing for healthcare workers.

Emergency Department Closures — The Public Face of the Crisis

🚨
Active Emergency

Northern Health's own website carries a standing alert: "Due to temporary service interruptions, some emergency departments may be closed." This disclaimer exists not because of equipment failures or natural disasters — it exists because there are not enough staff to keep the doors open.

Emergency departments across Northern Health's network — including Dawson Creek & District Hospital — have experienced repeated unplanned closures due to nursing and physician vacancies. A single unfilled shift can force a department to close, sending patients on 60–120+ minute drives for emergency care.

Northern Health operational data, 2023–2025

The Peace River region — served by the Dawson Creek & District Hospital — is home to over 65,000 residents, including rural communities with zero access to alternative care when the emergency department is closed. The nearest alternative acute care facilities are Fort St. John (75 km) or Prince George (395 km).

Rural and remote emergency transports cost $5,000–$25,000 per transport. When emergency departments close, patients who could be stabilized locally are transported at enormous cost — financial and human — to distant facilities. Every closure generates this cascading cost.

The Deficit Spiral: When Vacancies Become a Financial Crisis

Northern Health's growing operating deficit is not a mystery. It is a direct mathematical consequence of unresolved staffing shortages. The board has documented this progression explicitly across five years of meetings.

FY21–22
Near-zero operating deficit. Pandemic supplemental funding absorbs early strain. Workforce challenges noted but framed as "pandemic-related."
FY22–23
Deficit reaches $18.5M. Agency and overtime costs to fill nursing vacancies become the primary cost driver. Northern Health launches Housing Prototype Program in response. The problem is named.
FY23–24
Deficit temporarily recovered through Ministry adjustments. But structural vacancy problem remains unresolved. GoHealth BC (travel nurse program) tripled in size to fill gaps. Housing Prototype Program continues, unscaled.
FY24–25
Deficit explodes to $39.2M — the largest in the five-year record. Agency and overtime costs cited explicitly as primary driver. Housing escalated to formal Board-level strategic priority in October 2025. The prototype approach has not delivered at scale. NH needs a partner.

"We are spending exorbitant amounts on agency nurses because we have no structural solution. This is not sustainable."

— New Brunswick Health Minister, 2023 (mirroring identical language used internally across Northern Health's board deliberations)

The New Hospital Multiplies the Problem

⚠️
Counterintuitive But Critical

A new, 50% larger hospital does not solve the staffing crisis — it makes it significantly worse. Every new bed requires approximately 3–5 additional healthcare staff. The $433M Dawson Creek hospital replacement, opening in 2026–27, will require proportionally more traveling and locum staff than the facility it replaces — in a region with no new workforce housing.

The new Dawson Creek & District Hospital (DCDH) is 50% larger than the facility it replaces. It is built to LEED Gold certification and includes expanded acute care capacity for the Northeast region.

Northern Health Capital Reports, 2024–2025

As of late 2024, the building envelope is sealed and closed. Interior fit-out is underway. The hospital is expected to open in 2026–27. When it does, the demand for workforce housing — healthcare workers and construction completion crews alike — will spike significantly.

NH Board, December 2024 / 2025 capital reports

The hospital construction project alone — budget $377M–$433M — has generated an estimated $18 million in accommodation demand from construction workers, with an estimated 50% of the workforce traveling in from outside the region with no adequate accommodation.

ERS/NRCan Support Letter analysis, March 2026

The ERS Healthcare Housing Hub is located one block from the new Dawson Creek & District Hospital. This is not a coincidence — it is a deliberate, demand-matched deployment at the exact point of maximum need, timed to be operational as the new hospital opens.

Program Alignment

Alignment with the NRCan Offsite Construction Challenge

The Offsite Construction Challenge, under NRCan's Green Construction through Wood (GCWood) program, seeks to advance offsite prefabricated wood construction as a scalable, sustainable solution to Canada's housing and infrastructure needs. This project is purpose-built for that mandate.

Point-by-Point Program Alignment

✅
Offsite Prefabricated Wood Construction

The Hub is designed from the ground up as an offsite-manufactured, mass timber structure — BC-manufactured, factory-built, assembled on-site. This is the Offsite Construction Challenge's core purpose.

✅
Replicable Prototype

The Hub is explicitly designed as a replicable prototype — not a one-off project. The design, supply chain, and operational model are built to be exported to Fort St. John, Smithers, Terrace, and Prince George. Federal investment here is multiplied five times.

✅
Demonstrated Demand

The program seeks projects with real market demand. This project has pre-committed institutional demand from Northern Health, confirmed demand from a $433M hospital construction project, and documented 15+ years of community need.

✅
Rural and Remote Community Impact

NRCan's mandate includes applying innovative construction methods to serve underserved Canadian communities. Dawson Creek is precisely that — a remote northern community where conventional construction economics are not attractive to private developers without federal support.

✅
BC Forest Industry & Supply Chain Activation

BC-manufactured mass timber construction directly activates BC's forest products industry — a core economic pillar for Canada's natural resource sector and a direct NRCan mandate area.

✅
Environmental Performance

Mass timber offsite construction reduces on-site waste, lowers embodied carbon, and achieves superior GHG performance compared to conventional construction. The new DCDH hospital was built to LEED Gold — the Hub is designed to match or exceed this standard.

✅
Critical Social Infrastructure

Healthcare workforce housing directly supports hospital operations. It is, in the most literal sense, the infrastructure behind the infrastructure. Federal support here serves dual mandates — housing and healthcare — in one project.

✅
Leveraged Financing — Federal Anchor Effect

NRCan support is the critical anchor that validates the project to regional funders: NDIT (Northern Development Initiative Trust), McKenzie Trust, Community Futures, and Lakeview Credit Union. Federal participation unlocks a capital stack that cannot be assembled without it.

The Federal Logic: Why This is a National Investment

Canadian hospitals used 7.8 million agency hours in FY2023–24 — equivalent to 4,010 full-time traveling healthcare workers. Rural and remote purchased hours increased 261% since 2019–20. Every purpose-built workforce housing hub reduces this dependence.

CIHI State of the Health Workforce in Canada, 2024

Research from the Rural and Remote Health Journal found that housing support — quality accommodation, professionally managed, with community embedded — was associated with 35–45% improved staff retention at two-year follow-up, outperforming cash incentives. The most cost-effective retention tool is a bed and a door.

BC has $6.4B+ in new hospital construction underway in 2025. Every new hospital opening increases traveling staff demand. Without workforce housing infrastructure, new hospitals will face the same staffing crises as the facilities they replace. Federal investment in housing infrastructure now prevents a predictable crisis later.
