Executive Summary

Northern Health Is in a Structural Financial Crisis Driven by Workforce Costs — and Housing Is the Missing Piece

Northern Health (NH) has undergone a dramatic financial deterioration between FY2022 and FY2025. Total expenses grew from $1.126B to $1.688B — a 50% increase in three years. In FY2024-25, NH recorded an annual operating deficit of $16.1M (audited) against a projected shortfall of $39.2M per board minutes. The gap between these figures reflects mid-year projections versus final audited results, with emergency Ministry of Health supplemental funding likely bridging the difference in prior years.

The root cause is not hard to find: "Purchased services — other" (agency/contracted nursing) more than doubled from $57.5M (FY2023) to $101.8M (FY2025). GoHealth BC travel and accommodation expenses exploded 215% in a single year — from $4.3M to $13.5M. These are the direct costs of placing workers in hotels rather than homes.

ERS's thesis is validated by NH's own published numbers: stable housing for healthcare workers is not a soft benefit — it is the single highest-ROI intervention available to Northern Health's finance team right now.

⚠ Expense Overrun vs. Budget — FY2025

FY2025 total expenses exceeded budget by $34.8M (2.1% over). Acute care alone overran by $35.7M (+3.9% vs. budget of $904.9M). Corporate services overran by $18.6M (+14.4%). These are not rounding errors — they are structural cost pressure, primarily driven by agency staffing.

📊 Revenue Actually Beat Budget — The Problem Is Costs

NH revenues in FY2025 were $18.8M above budget. The deficit is not a revenue problem. Ministry of Health contributions came in $24.3M below budget, but MSP, recoveries, and other revenues more than offset it. NH's financial problem is entirely on the cost side — and agency staffing is driving it.

What GoHealth BC Actually Is

GoHealth BC is NH's answer to the permanent vacancy crisis — a flexible, rapid-deployment temporary staffing program. It fills immediate clinical gaps. But it doesn't solve the housing problem. GoHealth nurses arrive, work their rotations, and return home — often because there's no viable housing to stay in. The program is structurally designed to perpetuate temporary reliance rather than convert temporary staff to permanent community members.

The ERS Wedge: Housing Converts GoHealth to Permanent

If ERS can house GoHealth BC staff in stable, affordable, community-connected homes, the conversion rate from GoHealth placement to permanent hire should increase materially. Each conversion reduces agency hours, reduces hotel costs, and reduces GoHealth program reliance. The financial math is direct: one $105/night ERS unit vs. $200+ hotel + 2–3× staff cost = measurable, auditable savings NH's board can track.

NH is currently spending $101.8M/year on purchased services — other (primarily agency/contracted nursing) and $13.5M on GoHealth BC travel and accommodation alone. At $105/night per ERS unit vs. $200+/night hotel average, the accommodations cost differential is immediate and measurable.
